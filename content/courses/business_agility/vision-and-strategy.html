Why Vision Matters

In VUCA environments, vision provides:

Inspiration

Motivates teams to stretch beyond current capabilities

Engagement

Creates emotional connection to the work

Commitment

Builds dedication even when challenges arise

Cohesion

Aligns diverse teams around common direction

Remember VUCA from Module 1: Volatility, Uncertainty, Complexity, Ambiguity. In turbulent environments, vision is the anchor. Without vision, teams default to short-term, reactive thinking. With vision, teams can make decisions autonomously aligned to larger purpose. Source: ICAgile learning outcomes. Ask: "Think about a time you were part of team with compelling vision vs one without - what was different?"

What Vision Is (and Isn't)

Vision IS:

  • A clear picture of a desired future state
  • Inspiring and aspirational
  • A guide for decision-making
  • Focused on the "what" (what we want to create)

Vision is NOT:

  • Mission (why we exist)
  • Strategy (how we'll get there)
  • Goals (specific measurable targets)
  • Tactics (specific actions)
Many organizations confuse these terms. Vision: Where are we going? What does success look like? Mission: Why do we exist? What's our fundamental purpose? Strategy: How will we achieve the vision? What's our approach? Goals: What specific, measurable outcomes mark progress? Example: Amazon vision "To be Earth's most customer-centric company" vs their strategy (logistics, tech, marketplace).
Use CRISP as a checklist for evaluating vision statements. Crisp: "Be the best" is vague; "Transform how HR delivers value through data-driven insights" is crisp. Realistic: Should stretch capabilities but not feel impossible. Inspiring: Should make people excited to come to work. Specific: Should help answer "should we do this project?" questions. Purposeful: Should connect to impact that matters. We'll use these criteria in our practice exercise.

Vision Enables Decentralized Decision-Making

Traditional Approach

Leadership makes all strategic decisions → slow, bottlenecked

With Clear Vision

Teams make decisions aligned to vision → fast, scalable

The Connection: Vision acts as guardrails for autonomous teams

This is critical for business agility. When vision is clear and shared, you can trust teams to make good decisions. Teams ask: "Does this move us toward our vision?" instead of "What does my boss want?" Example: If marketing vision is "Create experiences customers love to share," team can decide whether to invest in viral campaign feature. Clarity = Speed. Ask: "What decisions could your team make faster if you had clearer vision?"

Example: Marketing Department Vision

Weak:

"Be a world-class marketing organization"

Strong:

"Create marketing experiences so valuable that customers seek them out, share them enthusiastically, and see us as trusted partners in their success"

Why it's better:

  • ✓ Specific behaviors (seek out, share, trust)
  • ✓ Customer-focused
  • ✓ Guides decisions (does this create value customers want to share?)
  • ✓ Inspires (aim for partner status, not just vendor)
Notice how the strong version gives clear decision criteria. "Should we do this email blast?" → "Would customers seek this out and share it?" "Should we sponsor this event?" → "Will this build trusted partner perception?" The weak version could mean anything - doesn't guide action. Invite participants to critique: what makes it compelling to them? Time check: 15 minutes elapsed, 30 to go.

"Modernize HR to support business growth"

Strong:

"Transform HR from administrative function to strategic partner that unlocks human potential, enabling every employee to do their best work and every leader to build high-performing teams"

  • ✓ Inspiring purpose (human potential)

Example: Finance Vision

"Provide accurate financial reporting and control"

"Become the trusted insight partner that empowers business leaders to make faster, smarter decisions through real-time financial clarity and predictive analytics"

Why it's better:

  • ✓ Relationship change (control → partner)
  • ✓ Business outcome focus (faster, smarter decisions)
  • ✓ Specific capabilities (real-time, predictive)
  • ✓ Shifts from backward-looking to forward-looking
Traditional finance = scorekeepers looking backward. Agile finance = strategic partners enabling speed. "Faster, smarter decisions" is outcome that business leaders care about. "Real-time" and "predictive" suggest specific capability investments. Ask: "Finance professionals in room - how would this change your prioritization?"

Customer Empathy and Value Creation

Why Customer Empathy Matters

Value is subjective

  • What's valuable to one customer may not be to another
  • Value depends on context, timing, needs, and alternatives
  • We can't define value - only customers can

Customer focus is a core capability for business agility

  • Enables fast feedback and learning
  • Reduces waste (building wrong things)
  • Builds loyalty and advocacy
Source: ICAgile learning outcomes emphasize value is subjective. Example: Same hotel room - business traveler values wifi, vacationer values pool, parent values adjoining rooms. Traditional approach: We define value based on features we build. Agile approach: We discover value by understanding customers. Ask: "When have you built something you thought was valuable but customers didn't use it?" This section gives tools to prevent that.

What is Customer Empathy?

Customer Empathy: Deep understanding of customer's context, needs, pains, and aspirations

It's not:

  • Surveys and statistics (those are data)
  • Assuming you know because you're like them
  • Focus groups where people say what they think you want to hear

It is:

  • Direct observation and conversation
  • Understanding their world, not just your product
  • Seeing through their eyes
Empathy = "feeling with" not "feeling for" (sympathy). Requires getting out of your own perspective. Example: Watching someone use your website vs asking "do you like our website?" Empathy precedes innovation - can't solve problems you don't understand. Ask: "Who are your customers - internal or external?"

Internal vs External Customers

External Customers:

End users who consume your product/service

Internal Customers:

Everyone between you and the end customer who depends on your work

Key Insight: If you're not directly serving external customers, your internal customers are just as important

Most people in this room likely have internal customers (other departments). HR customers: Employees and leaders. Finance customers: Business unit leaders, executives. Marketing customers: Might be both external and internal. IT customers: Internal users of systems. Your internal customers' success contributes to external customer success. "We all win together" - value chain thinking. Ask: "Who depends on your work? List 3 internal customers."
These are complementary tools, not alternatives. Persona: Understand who (demographics, goals, context). Empathy Map: Understand inner world (thoughts, feelings, motivations). Journey Map: Understand experience over time (touchpoints, emotions, pain points). Value Proposition: Align what we offer to what they need. All four together create comprehensive understanding. Time check: 10 minutes into Section 2.

Tool 1: Persona Maps

Purpose: Create semi-fictional representation of ideal customer

Key Elements:

  • Name, role, demographics
  • Goals and motivations
  • Pain points and frustrations
  • Context and constraints
  • How they make decisions

Example (HR): "Sarah, Department Manager - Needs to hire quickly but struggles with slow approval processes. Values efficiency and candidate experience. Makes decisions based on time-to-hire metrics."

Personas make abstract "users" concrete and memorable. "What would Sarah think?" is easier than "what would users think?" Should be based on research, not assumptions. Include photo and quote to make them feel real. Keep 2-3 primary personas, not 20. Use in meetings: "Does this serve Sarah's needs?" Template will be in take-home materials.

Tool 2: Empathy Maps

Purpose: Understand what customer thinks, feels, says, does

Four Quadrants:

  • Think & Feel: Inner world, worries, aspirations
  • See: What they observe in environment
  • Say & Do: Public behavior and statements
  • Hear: What others tell them

Bottom Section:

  • Pains: Frustrations, obstacles, fears
  • Gains: Wants, needs, success measures
Created by XPLANE, popularized by Strategyzer. Helps get inside customer's head. Think/Feel vs Say/Do reveals gaps (what they won't admit publicly). See and Hear provide context that shapes their perspective. Pains and Gains summarize what matters most. Best done based on actual interviews or observations. We'll practice this tool in our activity. Visual: Show empathy map template.

Tool 3: Customer Journey Maps

Purpose: Visualize customer's experience over time

Elements:

  • Stages: Phases of experience (Awareness → Consideration → Purchase → Use → Advocacy)
  • Touchpoints: Where they interact with you
  • Emotions: How they feel at each stage (emotional curve)
  • Pain Points: Where things go wrong
  • Opportunities: Where you could improve

Example (HR): New employee onboarding journey from job offer to 90-day mark

Journey maps reveal disconnects between stages. Example: Marketing creates excitement, but onboarding is disappointing. Emotional curve is key - where do they get frustrated or delighted? Often reveals that pain points are in transitions between stages. Example: HR onboarding gap between offer acceptance and first day (lost excitement). Should include both what IS happening and what you WANT to happen. Cross-functional tool - reveals handoff problems. Template in materials.

Tool 4: Value Proposition Canvas

Purpose: Align what you offer with what customers need

Customer Side (Profile):

  • Customer jobs (tasks they want to accomplish)
  • Pains (obstacles and frustrations)
  • Gains (desired outcomes and benefits)

Value Side (Map):

  • Products/Services (what you offer)
  • Pain relievers (how you address pains)
  • Gain creators (how you create gains)

Success = Fit between profile and map

Created by Strategyzer (Alex Osterwalder). Left side: Understand customer deeply. Right side: Design value proposition to match. Fit = your pain relievers match their pains, your gain creators match their gains. Common mistake: Starting with what we offer instead of what they need. Example: Customer job "evaluate vendor options quickly" → your pain reliever "free trial, no credit card needed". Tool helps test: Are we solving problems they actually have? Template available in Business Model Generation book and online.

Quick Example: Finance Team Empathy Map

Scenario: Business unit leader as internal customer of finance

Think & Feel:

"I need budget answers fast to make decisions, but finance takes weeks"

See:

Competitors moving faster, opportunities slipping away

Say & Do:

Asks for budget updates repeatedly, workarounds finance with assumptions

Hear:

CEO asking for faster decision-making, finance saying "we need complete data"

Pains:

Gains:

This is what empathy reveals - the gap between finance and business leader perspectives. Business leader sees finance as bottleneck, not partner. Finance might see themselves as ensuring accuracy and control. Empathy map helps finance understand the customer experience. Connect to vision: "trusted insight partner for faster decisions" addresses these pains/gains. This is preparation for our practice activity. Time check: 20 minutes into section 2.

Traditional Planning vs Backcasting

Traditional (Forecasting):

Start with current state → project forward → see where you end up

Backcasting:

Define desired future state → work backward → identify path to get there

Why backcasting is powerful:

  • More creative (not constrained by current limitations)
  • More motivating (inspiring future pulls you forward)
  • More strategic (focused on destination, not just incremental progress)
Forecasting asks "Where are current trends taking us?" Backcasting asks "Where do we want to be?" then "What needs to change?" Example: Forecasting = "We'll have 10% more automation by next year". Backcasting = "In 3 years we're fully automated, working backwards what capabilities do we need?" Backcasting especially valuable in complex, uncertain environments. Source: ICAgile learning outcomes. Ask: "Which approach do you typically use in your planning?"
Step 1 is where vision comes in - specific, compelling picture. Step 2 is capability gap analysis - what do we need that we don't have? Step 3 is roadmap creation - turning aspiration into plan. Key: Don't need to plan every detail, just major capability shifts. Example: Future state "real-time financial insights" → need "automated data pipeline" → need "data architecture redesign" → can start "current state data audit". Makes big visions feel achievable by breaking them down. Time check: 7 minutes into Section 3.

Example: Marketing Agility Future State

Future State (2 years):

"Marketing operates with 2-week iteration cycles, testing 10+ experiments per month, with real-time performance dashboards and autonomous team decision-making"

Capabilities Needed:

  • Rapid experimentation platform and processes
  • Data analytics infrastructure
  • Team skills in hypothesis testing
  • Decision-making authority and trust
  • Agile marketing framework adoption

Path Backward:

Q4 2027: Full autonomous operations
Q2 2027: All teams running experiments
Q4 2026: Platform launched, 2 pilot teams
Q2 2026: Skills training, platform selection
Q1 2026 (Now): Current state assessment, vision alignment

Notice how specific the future state is - measurable, observable. Capabilities span tech (platform), skills (testing), process (framework), culture (trust). Working backward creates sequence: trust before autonomy, platform before experiments. Quarterly milestones, not daily tasks - strategic not tactical. This becomes the roadmap that guides prioritization. Ask: "What would need to be in place for your team to operate more agilely?"

"HR delivers predictive insights on talent needs 6 months ahead, employees rate HR as their most valuable partner (90%+ satisfaction), leaders use HR data in every strategic decision"

  • Predictive modeling skills

Path shows major shifts: reactive → proactive, administrative → strategic. Working backward reveals: Can't jump to predictive without platform and skills. Roadmap helps communicate to stakeholders: "Here's why we need these investments". This is how you connect inspiring vision to practical steps.

Section 4 Overview

What is Foundations Sprint?

A time-boxed process to create strategic clarity through three phases:

  1. The Basics - Customer, problem, competition
  2. Differentiation - How you'll stand out
  3. Magic Lenses - How to evaluate approaches

Created by: Jake Knapp (Google Meet, Design Sprint) and John Zeratsky (Google Ventures)

Full version: 10 hours over 2 days
Today: 60-minute abbreviated version focused on core insights

Foundation Sprint source: Lenny's Podcast episode with Jake Knapp and JZ. Used with startups but highly applicable to any strategic initiative. Helps teams get clarity on what matters most. Core insight: Slow down to think clearly, so you can move faster long-term. Today we'll focus on key exercises that create strategic focus. Output: Clear understanding of who you serve, how you differentiate, and how to evaluate options. Time: 60 minutes including intro and debrief.

Why Foundations Sprint Matters

The Problem:

"Most teams rush to build without clarity on basics, creating generic solutions that don't stand out"

The Solution:

Slow down for strategic thinking → create radical clarity → move faster with confidence

Key Insight from Jake Knapp:
"AI makes building fast, but products become generic. Slowing down for deep thinking makes you faster long-term."

Source: Video timestamp 0:40-1:01. Common pattern: Teams skip strategy, jump to execution, realize later it's wrong. Foundation Sprint prevents this by front-loading strategic clarity. 10 hours seems slow, but saves months of building wrong thing. For us: 60 minutes of strategic thinking will clarify next steps. Ask: "How many of you have worked on projects where you later realized you were building the wrong thing?" Foundation Sprint prevents that.
These three phases build on each other. Basics = foundation and warm-up. Differentiation = heart of the sprint, where most value comes from. Magic Lenses = decision-making framework. Together they create "founding hypothesis" - your strategic thesis to test. Original sprint: ~3 hours basics, ~4 hours differentiation, ~2 hours lenses, ~1 hour synthesis. Today: Abbreviated but covers key concepts. Let's dive into each phase.

How We'll Work: "Work Alone Together"

The Method:

  1. Silent individual work - Everyone writes their own ideas (no talking!)
  2. Vote - Everyone places dots on ideas they like
  3. Decider - One person makes final call

Why it works:

  • Gets diverse perspectives (not groupthink)
  • Moves through decisions quickly
  • Saves decision-making energy
  • Introverts and extroverts equally heard
Source: Video timestamp 21:17-22:14. This is key facilitation method throughout Foundation Sprint. Silence is critical - prevents loudest voice from dominating. Voting surfaces patterns without long debate. Decider role (usually team lead or sponsor) breaks ties and maintains momentum. Example: "Who's the customer?" - everyone writes their answer silently, then we vote, then decider confirms. Feels awkward at first but creates better decisions faster. We'll practice this throughout activity. Time check: 10 minutes into Section 4, ready to start activity.

Activity Setup: Form Groups and Choose Scenario

Setup Instructions:

Group Size: 4-6 people

Choose a scenario to work on:

  • A new initiative or project starting soon
  • An existing product/service you want to improve
  • A strategic challenge or opportunity you're facing
  • A transformation you're planning

Designate a Decider: Usually team lead, project sponsor, or most senior person

You'll need: Sticky notes, markers, voting dots, template worksheets

Let's begin!

Give groups 2-3 minutes to form and choose scenario. Encourage real scenarios they care about - more engagement. Remind: Choose something with clear customer and competition. Decider role is important - should be someone with decision authority. Check: Does each group have supplies? Distribute: Basics worksheet, Differentiation worksheet, 2x2 chart template. Facilitator should circulate during activity to keep groups on time. Time check: Setup should take 3-5 minutes max.

Phase 1: The Basics (20 minutes)

⏱ 20 minutes

Answer these questions using "Work Alone Together":

  1. Who is your most important customer? (3 min)
  2. What problem are you solving for them? (3 min)
  3. Who is the competition? (3 min)
  4. How do they solve it today? (Alternatives/workarounds) (3 min)
  5. What are YOUR advantages? (Insight, motivation, special capabilities) (5 min)

For each question:
2 min silent writing → 30 sec voting → 30 sec decider confirms

START NOW!

Time each question strictly - use timer. Announce: "Silent writing - 2 minutes, go!" Then: "Put dots on your top choices - 30 seconds!" Then: "Decider, what's your call?" Keep energy high and pace fast. Circulate: Are groups being specific enough? Common issue: "Customer" too broad → push for specific segment. Source: Video 14:54-15:41 - "Almost embarrassingly simple but teams have different answers". By end you should have clear 1-sentence answers to each question. Advantages are foundation for differentiation - capture all ideas. Time check: 20 minutes for full Basics phase.

Phase 2: Differentiation - Introduction (2 minutes)

Now the heart of the Foundation Sprint:

"People's default is to ignore new things. You need RADICAL DIFFERENTIATION to break through."
— Jake Knapp

Your challenge:
Create a clear promise from customer's perspective that sets you apart from all alternatives

Key requirement:
You must be able to deliver AND customers must care

Source: Video 29:46-30:48. This is most important phase - where real strategic clarity comes from. Differentiation must be: 1. From customer's perspective (not "we use AI" but "you get answers 10x faster"), 2. Specific and measurable, 3. Something you can actually deliver, 4. Something customers actually value (not just cool to you). We'll use two tools: Classic differentiators + Custom differentiators. Then create 2x2 chart to visualize positioning. Time: 25 minutes for full differentiation phase.

Classic Differentiators Checklist (5 minutes)

⏱ 5 minutes

Rate yourself and competitors on these continuums (1-5):

  • Fast ↔ Slow
  • Smart ↔ Not so smart
  • Easy to use ↔ Hard to use
  • Free ↔ Expensive
  • Focused ↔ One size fits all
  • Simple ↔ Complicated
  • Integrated ↔ Siloed

Which 1-2 can you be RADICALLY better at?

Source: Video 31:31-32:14, 39:07-39:34. Give groups 5 minutes to score themselves and 2-3 main competitors. Key: Be honest - you don't need to win on everything. Look for where you can be notably better (4-5 points different). Example: Competitor is 2/5 on "fast", you could be 5/5 → good differentiator. Caution: Don't claim 5/5 on everything - not credible. Push for realistic self-assessment. Source: Video 43:49-44:22 "Don't need to be best on ALL differentiators". Circle the 1-2 where you have biggest advantage. Supplies: Classic differentiators worksheet.

⏱ 10 minutes

Format: [Good state] ↔ [Bad state]

Examples from video:

  • Networked ↔ Siloed
  • Painless growth ↔ Labor-intensive growth
  • Works out of the box ↔ Requires setup
  • Mobile first ↔ Desktop only

Your turn: Create 3-5 custom differentiators and score them

Source: Video 32:08-32:48, 46:17-47:43. Custom differentiators are often more powerful than classic ones. They "create new lens on reality for customers". Think: What continuum would show you in best light? Example (HR): "Proactive talent insights ↔ Reactive hiring support". Example (Finance): "Real-time predictive ↔ Monthly backward-looking". Work alone together: 5 min brainstorm differentiators, 3 min score, 2 min vote on top 2. Look for differentiators that: 1. Customers care about (not just cool to you), 2. You can credibly claim advantage, 3. Competition can't easily copy. This creates your unique strategic positioning.

Create Your 2x2 Differentiation Chart (10 minutes)

⏱ 10 minutes

Position yourself against competitors

Choose your top 2 differentiators as axes:

  • Horizontal axis: Differentiator #1
  • Vertical axis: Differentiator #2

Plot competitors:

  • Where does each alternative land?
  • Goal: Put all competitors in "Loserville" (the L-shaped bottom-left area)
  • Put yourself in top-right

Test:

  • Can you actually deliver on this positioning?
  • Do customers care about these dimensions?
Source: Video 31:02-34:46. "Loserville" = the three quadrants that aren't top-right (video 34:02). This is the iconic 2x2 positioning chart. Reference: Steve Jobs iPhone intro slide - smartphone vs not smart, easy vs hard. Example: If your axes are "Fast vs Slow" and "Personalized vs Generic": Top-right: Fast + Personalized (you), Competitors might be: Fast + Generic, Slow + Personalized, Slow + Generic. Phase 3: Magic Lenses Introduction (10 minutes)

The Challenge:
Multiple approaches could solve the same problem for the same customer - how do you choose?

The Solution: Evaluate options through different "lenses"

Five Lenses:

  1. Customer: Easy to use vs perfect solution
  2. Pragmatic: Cheap and fast to market
  3. Growth: Reach most customers, easy to adopt
  4. Money: Long-term value, biggest audience
  5. Differentiation: Delivers on your positioning
Source: Video 54:54-56:27. This phase helps when you have multiple paths forward. Example: Should we build mobile app or web app first? Each lens reveals different priorities. Customer lens: Mobile might be easier to use. Pragmatic lens: Web might be faster to market. Growth lens: Mobile might reach more people. Money lens: Web might have better long-term value. Differentiation lens: Which delivers on our promise? Use lenses to score options, then discuss which lens matters most for this decision. Time: We'll do quick intro and demo, groups can apply to their scenarios.

Magic Lenses Quick Exercise

Apply the Magic Lenses

Think of 2-3 different approaches to your challenge

Score each approach (1-5) on each lens:

  • Customer lens
  • Pragmatic lens
  • Growth lens
  • Money lens
  • Differentiation lens

Discuss: Which lens matters most for this decision?

What does that suggest about your approach?

Give groups 7-8 minutes for this exercise. Example approaches (Marketing): Influencer campaign vs content marketing vs paid ads. Example approaches (HR): Build internal tool vs buy external platform vs hybrid. Scoring reveals trade-offs. Key question: Which lens is most important given your context and goals? If differentiation is "fast", then pragmatic lens (cheap/fast) might align. If differentiation is "perfect solution", customer lens matters most. This creates framework for decision-making going forward. No single right answer - clarity on priorities is the goal. Supplies: Magic lenses worksheet.

Appendix: Founding Hypothesis Template

If we solve [SPECIFIC PROBLEM]

for [SPECIFIC CUSTOMER]

with [SPECIFIC APPROACH]

we think they'll choose us over [SPECIFIC ALTERNATIVES]

because of [DIFFERENTIATOR 1] and [DIFFERENTIATOR 2]

Example:

If we solve slow, reactive financial reporting for business unit leaders with real-time predictive analytics dashboards, we think they'll choose us over traditional monthly reports because we're 10x FASTER and PREDICTIVE not reactive.

Mad libs format makes it simple to complete. Must be specific - no generic language. This becomes your strategic hypothesis to test. Share with stakeholders to align on direction. Use to guide prioritization: Does this work support our hypothesis? Revisit quarterly: Is hypothesis still valid based on what we've learned? Format source: Video 16:35-16:47.
