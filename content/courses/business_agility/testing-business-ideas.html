The DVF Framework

Desirability, Viability, Feasibility

Every business faces three fundamental questions:

üéØ Desirability

Do they want this?

Value proposition-customer fit

Failure mode: Great tech nobody wants

üí∞ Viability

Should we do this?

Can we capture value sustainably?

Failure mode: Loved product with no business model

‚öôÔ∏è Feasibility

Can we do this?

Technical, regulatory, operational ability

Failure mode: Perfect market fit but can't deliver

The intersection of all three = Sweet spot for success

Your Business Model as a System of Assumptions

The canvas isn't a checklist - it's a story of how you create, deliver, and capture value. Early on, every box contains guesses, not facts.

Front Stage (Customer-facing)

  • Customer Segments: Who are you serving?
  • Value Proposition: What are you offering?
  • Channels: How do you reach them?
  • Relationships: How do you interact?
  • Revenue Streams: How do you make money?

Back Stage (Operations)

  • Key Activities: What must you do?
  • Key Resources: What must you have?
  • Key Partnerships: Who will help?
  • Cost Structure: What will it cost?

Each element can have Desirability, Viability, or Feasibility risks

A Simple but Powerful Prioritization Tool

Two Dimensions Create Clarity

  • Vertical Axis: How important is this to our success?
  • Horizontal Axis: How much evidence supports this?

The Magic of Relative Positioning

  • Start with one assumption
  • Place each new one relative to others
  • Don't seek perfection - seek clarity

Upper right = Critical unknowns (test these first!)

Lower left = Minor details (test these last or never)

Knowledge Check: Modules 1 & 2

Question 1: DVF Framework

A startup built an amazing AI product that users love, but can't figure out how to monetize it. What type of risk did they fail to address?

  • A) Desirability risk
  • B) Viability risk
  • C) Feasibility risk
  • D) All of the above

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: They validated Desirability (users love it) and Feasibility (they built it), but failed to validate Viability (sustainable business model). This is a common failure mode for startups.

Question 2: Assumption Mapping

You have an assumption in the lower-left quadrant (low importance, strong evidence). What should you do?

  • A) Test it first - the evidence might be wrong
  • B) Test it last or not at all - focus resources elsewhere
  • C) Move it to the upper-right quadrant
  • D) Remove it from your canvas

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: Low importance + strong evidence = low priority. Focus your limited testing resources on critical unknowns (upper-right: high importance, low evidence). That's where the biggest risks live.

Discovery vs. Validation Experiments

Two Phases of Testing

üîç Discovery

Going from NONE to SOME evidence

  • Exploratory and qualitative
  • Understanding the problem space
  • Low cost, quick to run
  • Generates directional insights

Examples:

  • Customer interviews
  • Observation studies
  • Search trend analysis
  • Paper prototypes

‚úÖ Validation

Going from SOME to STRONG evidence

  • Quantitative and behavioral
  • Testing specific solutions
  • Higher cost, longer to run
  • Generates measurable proof

Examples:

  • Landing pages
  • MVPs
  • Concierge tests
  • Letters of intent

Discovery helps you aim. Validation pulls the trigger.

Case Study: BroBot Dating Advice

A Real NYC Startup Story

  • Men's dating advice chatbot
  • Text for personalized advice
  • Team of talented founders
  • Seemingly perfect market fit

Let's see how systematic testing saved them from disaster...

From Assumption to Evidence

Their Assumption Map Revealed:

  • "Men want dating advice" (Important, no evidence)
  • "They'll pay for instant access" (Important, no evidence)
  • "AI can provide good advice" (Important, no evidence)

Their Experiment Sequence:

1. Customer Interviews

15 men interviewed

Result: Yes, dating is hard, advice is overwhelming

2. Search Trend Analysis

Google Trends research

Result: Thousands searching "dating advice for men" monthly

3. Paper Prototype

Conversation flow testing

Result: Positive feedback on interaction model

4. Landing Page

Value prop test

Result: 1,000 signups in hours (!)

5. Wizard of Oz

3 women manually texting as "AI"

Result: Extremely high engagement

What They Learned

‚úÖ Desirability: VALIDATED

  • Overwhelming demand (too many signups!)
  • Men loved personalized, instant advice
  • Engagement was extremely high

‚ùì Feasibility: UNCERTAIN

  • Manual responses didn't scale
  • Real AI would take significant investment
  • Quality bar was very high

‚ùå Viability: FAILED

  • Zero willingness to pay
  • No clear monetization path
  • Acquisition costs > lifetime value

Decision: Shut down after 6 weeks

Cost to learn: ~$5,000

Cost avoided: ~$500,000+

100x ROI on testing investment

Key Lessons from BroBot

‚úÖ What Went Right

  • Tested before building
  • Used escalating experiments
  • Kept experiments small
  • Made decision on data

‚ö†Ô∏è What Could Improve

  • Test willingness to pay earlier
  • Set signup limits on experiments
  • Have scaling plan before Wizard of Oz

üí° The Big Insight

"People loving your product isn't enough if they won't pay for it"

Better to learn with $5K in experiments
than $500K in development

Knowledge Check: Modules 3 & 4

Question 3: Discovery vs Validation

You have NO evidence that enterprise buyers will pay $50K/year for your solution. What type of experiment should you run FIRST?

  • A) Build MVP and sell it
  • B) Customer interviews to understand budget authority
  • C) Landing page with pricing
  • D) Letter of Intent

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: Start with Discovery (interviews) when you have ZERO evidence. Learn about procurement processes, budget cycles, and decision makers before running Validation experiments. Options A, C, D are all Validation experiments that require some baseline evidence first.

Question 4: BroBot Case Study

What was BroBot's critical mistake in their testing sequence?

  • A) They didn't test Desirability
  • B) They tested Viability (willingness to pay) too late
  • C) They used too many experiments
  • D) They shut down too early

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: They validated Desirability extensively but didn't test Viability (monetization) until late in the process. Testing willingness to pay earlier (e.g., "Would you pay $X?" in interviews or fake checkout page) would have revealed this fatal flaw sooner and cheaper.

Building Your 12-Week Testing Sprint

Time to put it all together. You'll create a 12-week testing plan that systematically addresses your critical unknowns through evidence-building experiments.

In this module:

  1. Design your first month of experiments
  2. Define success criteria
  3. Plan your pivot triggers

From Assumptions to Evidence in 12 Weeks

Week 1-4: Discovery Phase

  • Focus on #1 critical unknown
  • Run 2-3 discovery experiments
  • Aim for directional evidence

Week 5-8: Early Validation

  • Test refined hypothesis
  • Run 1-2 validation experiments
  • Seek behavioral evidence

Week 9-12: Deep Validation

  • Test full solution
  • Run MVP-level experiment
  • Generate investment-grade evidence

Remember: Each phase builds on previous learning

Evidence Progression: None ‚Üí Some ‚Üí Strong ‚Üí Investment-Ready

Watch Out For These Traps

‚ö†Ô∏è Validation Theater

Running experiments after you've already decided

Antidote: Be genuinely open to negative results

‚ö†Ô∏è Perfection Paralysis

Waiting for perfect evidence before moving

Antidote: "Strong enough" evidence is good enough

‚ö†Ô∏è Confirmation Bias

Only seeing data that supports your hypothesis

Antidote: Actively seek disconfirming evidence

‚ö†Ô∏è The Build Trap

Jumping to building after one positive signal

Antidote: Validate with multiple experiment types

‚ö†Ô∏è Analysis Paralysis

Testing forever without deciding

Antidote: Set decision deadlines (12-week sprints)

Remember: The goal isn't to run experiments. The goal is to de-risk your business.

Good experiments lead to hard decisions.
