What Vision Is (and Isn't)

Vision IS:

  • A clear picture of a desired future state
  • Inspiring and aspirational
  • A guide for decision-making
  • Focused on the "what" (what we want to create)

Vision is NOT:

  • Mission (why we exist)
  • Strategy (how we'll get there)
  • Goals (specific measurable targets)
  • Tactics (specific actions)

Example: Amazon's vision "To be Earth's most customer-centric company" vs their strategy (logistics, tech, marketplace)

Many organizations confuse these terms. Clarity on what vision IS helps you craft a better one. Vision: Where are we going? What does success look like? Mission: Why do we exist? What's our fundamental purpose? Strategy: How will we achieve the vision? What's our approach? Goals: What specific, measurable outcomes mark progress? Time: 2 minutes
These four outcomes build on each other. Customer clarity enables competitive positioning, which informs your approach, culminating in a testable hypothesis. Emphasize: Every successful business can articulate these four elements clearly. Time: 2 minutes Question to ask: "Which of these feels most challenging for your initiative right now?"

Module 1

The Basics

Customer, Problem, Competition, Advantages

Module 1 is about getting crystal clear on the fundamentals. These are the building blocks everything else rests on. Time: 30 seconds

The Foundation of Strategy

Customer

Who exactly are we serving?

  • Specific segments
  • Clear personas
  • Measurable criteria

Problem

What pain are we solving?

  • Urgent needs
  • Costly problems
  • Frequent frustrations

Competition

What alternatives exist?

  • Direct competitors
  • Workarounds
  • Status quo

Advantages

Why would they choose us?

  • Unique capabilities
  • Special insights
  • Unfair advantages
These four elements are non-negotiable. Every successful strategy can clearly articulate all four. Common mistake: Being too broad with customer definition. "Everyone" is not a customer segment. Time: 3 minutes

Our Method: Note and Vote

The Process

  1. Silent Generation (5 min)
    Everyone writes ideas on sticky notes
  2. Share & Post (5 min)
    Quick explanation as you post
  3. Dot Voting (2 min)
    Vote for the best ideas
  4. Discussion (3 min)
    Focus on top-voted items

Why It Works

  • Equal participation
  • Reduces groupthink
  • Surfaces best ideas quickly
  • Creates consensus
  • Time-boxed decisions

"The loudest voice in the room isn't always the smartest."

Note and Vote is our secret weapon. It democratizes idea generation and prevents the HIPPO effect (Highest Paid Person's Opinion). Facilitation tip: Be strict with time boxes. Use a visible timer. Time: 2 minutes

Why Customer Empathy Matters

Value is subjective

  • What's valuable to one customer may not be to another
  • Value depends on context, timing, needs, and alternatives
  • We can't define value - only customers can

Customer focus enables strategy

  • Fast feedback and learning
  • Reduces waste (building wrong things)
  • Builds loyalty and advocacy
  • Validates your differentiation matters

Without deep customer understanding, you're guessing - not strategizing

Example: Same hotel room - business traveler values wifi, vacationer values pool, parent values adjoining rooms. Traditional approach: We define value based on features we build. Strategic approach: We discover value by understanding customers deeply. Your differentiation must be valued by customers, not just clever to you. Time: 2 minutes

Understanding the Competitive Landscape

Direct Competitors

Companies solving the same problem

  • Similar solutions
  • Same target market
  • Comparable pricing

Indirect Competitors

Different solutions, same problem

  • Workarounds
  • Manual processes
  • Hybrid solutions

Status Quo

The "do nothing" option

  • Living with the problem
  • Current habits
  • Inertia

Remember: Your biggest competitor is often "doing nothing"

Most teams underestimate the power of status quo. Change is hard, and people need compelling reasons to switch. Ask the group: "Why would someone NOT buy any solution?" Time: 2 minutes

  • Automate everything possible

  • Add features that slow us down
  • Require lengthy implementations

Traditional Planning vs Backcasting

Traditional (Forecasting):

Start with current state â†’ project forward â†’ see where you end up

Backcasting:

Define desired future state â†’ work backward â†’ identify path to get there

Why backcasting is powerful:

  • More creative (not constrained by current limitations)
  • More motivating (inspiring future pulls you forward)
  • More strategic (focused on destination, not just incremental progress)
Forecasting asks "Where are current trends taking us?" Backcasting asks "Where do we want to be?" then "What needs to change?" Example: Forecasting = "We'll have 10% more automation by next year". Backcasting = "In 3 years we're fully automated, working backwards what capabilities do we need?" Backcasting especially valuable in complex, uncertain environments. Time: 3 minutes

The Six Magic Lenses

Evaluate your approach through multiple perspectives

1. Customer Expert Lens

Would someone who deeply understands the customer believe this approach solves their problem?

2. Pragmatic Lens

Can we realistically build and deliver this with our resources and timeline?

3. Growth Lens

Does this approach have potential for significant scale and expansion?

4. CFO/Money Lens

Do the unit economics work? Can we make money at scale?

5. Differentiation Lens

Does this approach reinforce our chosen differentiators?

6. Conviction Lens

Do we genuinely believe in this approach? Will we persist through challenges?

Each lens reveals potential weaknesses. An approach must pass all six to be viable. The conviction lens is often overlooked but critical - you need to believe in what you're building. Time: 5 minutes

Module 4

Founding Hypothesis

Your Testable Strategic Statement

This is where everything comes together into one clear, testable statement. Time: 30 seconds

Example: Strong Vision Statements

Weak Marketing Vision:

"Be a world-class marketing organization"

Strong Marketing Vision:

"Create marketing experiences so valuable that customers seek them out, share them enthusiastically, and see us as trusted partners in their success"

Why it's better:

  • Specific behaviors (seek out, share, trust)
  • Customer-focused outcome
  • Guides decisions: "Would customers seek this out and share it?"
  • Inspires: aim for partner status, not just vendor

Strong visions create decision criteria that guide daily work

Notice how the strong version gives clear decision criteria. "Should we do this email blast?" becomes "Would customers seek this out and share it?" The weak version could mean anything - doesn't guide action. This same principle applies to your founding hypothesis - be specific and actionable. Time: 2 minutes

The Founding Hypothesis Formula

Your Strategic Bet

If we solve [PROBLEM]
for [CUSTOMER]
with [APPROACH],
we believe they'll choose us over [COMPETITORS]
because of [DIFFERENTIATOR 1] and [DIFFERENTIATOR 2]

Why This Structure Works:

  • Forces clarity on all strategic elements
  • Creates testable assumptions
  • Aligns team on single vision
  • Guides all decisions going forward
This is your north star. Every decision should support or test this hypothesis. It's called a hypothesis because it's meant to be tested and refined. Time: 3 minutes

Activity: Draft Your Founding Hypothesis

Hypothesis Creation Workshop

Step 1: Fill in each element from previous modules (5 min)

Step 2: Refine for clarity and flow (5 min)

Step 3: Test and iterate (5 min)

Duration: 15 minutes

Example Hypothesis:

"If we solve the challenge of slow feature delivery for product managers at B2B SaaS companies with an AI-powered requirements generation platform, we believe they'll choose us over traditional project management tools because of our 10x speed advantage and deep SaaS-specific intelligence."

Work as a group to craft this. Read it aloud multiple times. Does it feel right? Common issue: Too wordy. Keep refining until it's crisp. Time: 15 minutes

From Hypothesis to Validation

Key Assumptions to Test:

  1. Customer has the problem
  2. Problem is worth solving
  3. Our approach works
  4. Differentiators matter
  5. They'll pay our price
  6. We can deliver profitably

Testing Methods:

  • Customer interviews
    Do they have this problem?
  • Prototype testing
    Does the approach work?
  • Pricing experiments
    Will they pay?
  • Competitive analysis
    Do differentiators matter?
The hypothesis is just the beginning. Now you need to systematically test each assumption. Start with the riskiest assumption first. Time: 3 minutes
