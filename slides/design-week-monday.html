AGILE METHODOLOGY

- ask if anyone can define it
- agile is designed to be iterative, incremental, evolutionary
- break product development work into small increments that minimize the amount of up-front planning and design. Iterations, or sprints, are short time frames (timeboxes) that typically last from one to four weeks.

Efficient communication & the PO

- every team should include a customer representative ("Product Owner" in Scrum). This person is agreed by stakeholders to act on their behalf and makes a personal commitment to being available for developers to answer questions throughout the iteration.

Short feedback loop and adaptive cycle

the daily stand-up (also known as the daily scrum). In a brief session, team members report to each other what they did the previous day toward their team's iteration goal, what they intend to do today toward the goal, and any roadblocks or impediments they can see to the goal.

Quality focus

Specific tools and techniques, such as continuous integration, automated unit testing, pair programming, test-driven development, design patterns, behavior-driven development, domain-driven design, code refactoring and other techniques are often used to improve quality and enhance product development agility.
-TESTING

SCRUM

- a sub-section of agile
- accepting that the problem cannot be fully understood or defined up front, and instead focusing on how to maximize the team's ability to deliver quickly, to respond to emerging requirements, and to adapt to evolving technologies and changes in market conditions.
- Values:
- Commitment: Team members individually commit to achieving their team goals, each and every sprint.
- Courage: Team members know they have the courage to work through conflict and challenges together so that they can do the right thing.
- Focus: Team members focus exclusively on their team goals and the sprint backlog; there should be no work done other than through their backlog.
- Openness: Team members and their stakeholders agree to be transparent about their work and any challenges they face.
- Respect: Team members respect each other to be technically capable and to work with good intent.

Scrum Master

-accountable for removing impediments to the ability of the team to deliver the product goals and deliverables.
-Helping the team to determine the definition of done for the product, with input from key stakeholders
-Coaching the team
-Promoting self-organization within the team
-Leading daily scrum
-Leading sprint planning, sprint reviews, retrospectives
-Motivating team

DevOps

- lead for code architecture - continuous integration and deployment

Quality Assurance (QA)

The quality assurance (QA) role is one that is focused on creating a quality deliverable. In other words, it is the responsibility of the QA role to make sure that the software development process doesn't sacrifice quality in the name of completed objectives.

UX/UI

-UX is focused on the user’s journey to solve a problem, UI is focused on how a product’s surfaces look and function Ken Norton, Google Ventures
- responsible for keeping the user's needs in mind (includes accessibility)

DESIGN THINKING

- this week we will be focussing on design thinking
- design thinking is a process for creative problem solving
- as it is user-centered, it encourages people to focus on the people that they are creating for
- it utilizes elements from the designer's toolkit like empathy and experimentation to arrive at innovative solutions.
- By using design thinking, you make decisions based on what future customers really want instead of relying only on historical data or making risky bets based on instinct instead of evidence.

user-centered design

- is a type of design thinking that uses a mixture of investigative methods & tools (eg. surveys & interview) and generative ones (eg. brainstorming) to develop an understanding of user needs.
- Avoiding this! You may think you’re creating something useful, impactful, efficient BUT if you don’t actually understand your users - who they are and what they want - then you will just create something no one uses. (courtesy of CAST)

THE THREE PILLARS

Empathy

- empathy: understanding the needs of your users

Ideation

- ideation: generating a lot of ideas! brainstorming is one example, but there are many ways. You will learn some of these throughout the week

Experimentation

- experimentation: testing your ideas with prototyping

A ROADMAP

- Design thinking provides a roadmap for you to use when developing new services
- It relies on the idea of iteration - things can always be improved
- Start by identifying a driving question that inspires you and your team to think about who you’re really designing for, and what they actually need
- Next, you gather inspiration—what other solutions out in the world can help you rethink the way you’re working?
- Use that to push past obvious solutions, and arrive at breakthrough ideas.
- Build rough prototypes to make those ideas tangible, and find what’s working and what’s not.
- Gather feedback, go back to the drawing board, and keep going.
- Some of those steps may happen several times, and you may even jump back and forth between them.

DAY ONE

Today, you’ll kick off your sprint by sharing knowledge, understanding the problem, and choosing a target for the week’s efforts. If you don’t first slow down, share what you know, and prioritize, you could end up wasting time and effort on the wrong part of the problem. Today's structured discussions create a path for the sprint week. In the morning, you’ll start at the end and agree to a long-term goal. Next, you’ll make a map of the challenge. In the afternoon, you’ll talk to some potential users who may be able to give you additional insight. Finally, you’ll pick a target: an ambitious but manageable piece of the problem that you can solve in one week.

What's your long-term goal?

15 minutes

- Revisit the initial project brief.
- What is the big problem? What is the overarching goal that you are trying to achieve?
- Can think of this in the 'tweet' format that students use in the discovery workshop - Write this on your white board.

Get pessimistic

15 minutes

- Looking at your goal, what problems might you encounter?
- What questions do you have about the project? How might the idea fail?
- List these problems on your white board
- Note that you can continue to update these moving forward

Map out your process

15 minutes

- Think about the process that a user would need to undergo to reach your long-term goal.
- How would a user interact with your product? What steps would they need to take?
- What is your main user journey?
- Create a flow chart that maps this process from beginning to goal (should be no more than 5-15 steps!) - Example: An app that helps you find a restaurant 1. Person is hungry & wants to eat somewhere delicious and close by 2. Open an app on their phone 3. App finds nearby restaurants which include info about the restaurant and customer reviews 4. App provides user with directions on how to go to restaurant 5. Person goes to that restaurant and eats.

BREAK

15 minutes

- ask people to be mindful of time, as we will be moving quickly through the material

How might we? (solo)

15 minutes

- Grab Post-its and Sharpies and begin framing questions that you might have about your user journey.
- Each question should start with, “How might we…” - you can write 'HMW' at the top of your post-its
- Work silently

Amp up the good: HMW use the kids’ energy to entertain fellow passengers?

Remove the bad: HMW separate the kids from fellow passengers?

Explore the opposite: HMW make the wait the most exciting part of the trip?

Go after adjectives: HMW we make the rush refreshing instead of harrying?

Create an analogy from need or context: HMW make the airport like a spa? Like a playground?

Play against the challenge: HMW make the airport a place that kids want to go?

Change a status quo: HMW make playful, loud kids less annoying?

Break POV into pieces: HMW entertain kids? HMW slow a mom down? HMW mollify delayed passengers?

How might we? (group)

15 minutes

- Come together and share your HMWs, group them into themes - Take some time to come up with some new ones together - At the end, do a silent vote where each person puts a dot on the 3 HMWs they see as most important

Solutions

15 minutes

- Take 15 minutes to silently and independently brainstorm solutions to your HMWs - Start with the HMW with the most votes - Write each solution on a post-it note

The cream of the crop

15 minutes

- Discuss your solutions together
- Is there overlap? What are the themes? Are their new solutions you can come up with as a group?
- Take a silent vote where everyone puts a dot beside their 2 favourite solutions.

Pick a target

30 minutes

- Update your map to include your top solutions
- Decide on your most important user journey and circle it (take a picture!)
- Start thinking scope (is this something that you can imagine completing in your given time frame)
- Can the problem/solution be broken down further?
- Remember that although it feels like you have a lot more time on these projects, its often better to choose a scope of project comparable to the projects that you have completed already. This will give you more time to consider things in front-end design (UX/UI), testing, etc.

LUNCH

Please be prompt coming back - we have a lot to get through!

User personas

15 minutes

- As a group decide on who you think your main user is
- Fill out your user persona form
- Empathize with them. What do they dream of? What does their environment look like? What is their daily routine like?
- Next slide gives some prompts

Assumptions

7 minutes for independent brainstorming

7 minutes for group theming / additions

- Take 7 minutes to silently list out the assumptions that you are making about your user on post it notes
- Use 7 minutes to discuss your assumptions and group them into themes

Create your user survey

15 minutes

6-8 open-ended questions that test your assumptions

No leading questions

Don't ask if they like, would use or would buy your product

Ask them about specific events in their past

Create a script to follow

15 minutes

- Introduce who you are and what you are doing
- Introduce your project
- Potentially offer a free coffee or other incentive
- Start with a bit of information about the person
- Age, occupation, use of technology, general feelings about the project themes
- Use the script as a guideline, not a bible

User research

2 hours

- Split up into teams of two
- Each team should find a minimum of 2-3 people to speak to over the next 2 hours
- this can be done remotely or in-person (from Space4, Starbucks, the street, library, grocery store etc - think about locations where you might find your user)
- One person should act as the note taker, while the other person is the interviewer
- Make the interviewer feel comfortable
- Think about your environment - is there a place that you can take your interviewee to that is less distracting? Noisy?

Synthesize your results

1 hours

Update your goal, map, target

- Return to Space4 and discuss your findings
- What did you learn? How can you integrate this into your map and potential solution?
- Update your goal, map and target
