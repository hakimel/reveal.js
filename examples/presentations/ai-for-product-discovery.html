Part 1

Customer Problem Discovery

Customer Problem Discovery

Part 1 of 5

Goal

Surface customer pain points from quotes, tickets, and feedback—then refine and prioritize two problem statements to carry forward.

How It Works

  1. Prompt AI to list top pain points from pasted snippets
  2. De-duplicate and rewrite as concise problem statements
  3. Prioritize the two most important to address

Tools: AI assistant (ChatGPT, Claude, etc.) + Mural "Problem Discovery" board

Activity: Problem Mining

⏱ 15 minutes

In Pairs: Discover Pain Points

Instructions:

  1. Paste 3-4 customer quotes or support tickets into your AI tool
  2. Prompt: "List the top 5 customer pain points or unmet needs from these quotes"
  3. Capture each pain point as a sticky note in Mural's "Problem Discovery" board
  4. Label each with the quote snippet for traceability

Example: NovaFit Feedback

"I start strong but lose motivation after 2 weeks. I need something that adapts when I fall off track."

"The app expects me to work out 5 days a week. Who has time for that?"

AI Output Example

  • Pain point: Motivation drops after initial phase
  • Pain point: Time commitment unrealistic for target users

Activity: Synthesis & Prioritization

⏱ 15 minutes

Refine and Select Top Problems

Instructions:

  1. Review all pain points on your Mural board
  2. Merge duplicates—cluster similar themes
  3. Rewrite top themes as clear problem statements (1-2 sentences)
  4. Prioritize using: Frequency (how often mentioned) × Severity (impact on user experience)
  5. Select your top two problem statements to carry forward

Example Problem Statements

  • Problem 1: Users struggle to maintain motivation beyond initial 2-3 weeks, especially when life disrupts their routine.
  • Problem 2: Target users (busy professionals 24-45) cannot commit to rigid 5-day workout schedules and need flexible, time-efficient options.

Key Takeaways: Problem Discovery

AI speeds pattern mining: Analyzing dozens of quotes manually is time-consuming; AI surfaces themes in seconds.

Human review adds nuance: AI may miss context, cultural subtleties, or emerging weak signals. Your domain expertise catches what AI cannot.

Bias mitigation is critical: If your qualitative data lacks diversity (e.g., no input from underrepresented users), AI will amplify that gap. Always audit your data sources.

Prioritization guides validation: Selecting top problems focuses your limited resources on high-impact opportunities. Document your rationale for future reference.

Reflection Questions

  1. What patterns did AI reveal that you might have missed manually?
  2. Where did you override or refine AI's suggestions? Why?
  3. How will you validate these problem statements with real users?

Build a diverse Idea Bank with AI-powered brainstorming

AI-Driven Idea Generation

Part 2 of 5

Goal

Generate a diverse Idea Bank spanning MVP features, radical concepts, UX patterns, business-model tweaks, and distribution channels.

The Process

  1. Draft targeted prompts for different idea categories
  2. Run prompts and capture all outputs in your Idea Bank
  3. Iterate prompts to push into unexplored territories
  4. Highlight 3-4 most novel or surprising ideas

Prompt Library: Idea Generation

Use these prompts with NovaFit's problems, or adapt for your own context:

MVP Features:
"Brainstorm 8 MVP features to solve [Problem 1: motivation drop-off]. Label each as either MVP or experiment."
Radical Concepts:
"Suggest 5 radical product concepts that address [Problem 2: time constraints] in novel ways—not just features, but reimagined approaches."
UX & Business Models:
"Iterate: surface UX patterns, business-model tweaks, and channel experiments for [combined problems]. Think beyond the app itself."

Pro Tips

  • Be specific: Reference your actual problem statements
  • Request diversity: Explicitly ask for "novel," "unconventional," or "outside the box"
  • Iterate: If first output is too conservative, ask AI to "go further" or "challenge assumptions"

Activity: Build Your Idea Bank

⏱ 40 minutes total

Prompt Crafting + Idea Generation + Highlighting

Phase 1 (10 min): Craft Your Prompts

  • Choose 2-3 prompts from the library or write your own
  • Customize with your problem statements from Part 1

Phase 2 (20 min): Generate Ideas

  • Run each prompt through your AI tool
  • Capture all ideas in Mural "Idea Bank" board
  • Aim for 20-30 total ideas across categories

Phase 3 (10 min): Highlight Novelty

  • Tag 3-4 ideas that surprised you or pushed boundaries
  • Note why each is interesting (even if not immediately feasible)

Key Takeaways: Idea Generation

Prompt quality widens idea space: Generic prompts yield generic ideas. Targeted, iterative prompts unlock novel solutions you wouldn't reach solo.

Scale and speed from AI: Generating 30 ideas manually might take hours across multiple brainstorm sessions. AI delivers in minutes, letting you focus energy on evaluation.

Iteration matters: First AI output is rarely optimal. Push back, ask "what if," challenge constraints. The conversation with AI is where breakthrough ideas emerge.

Human filtering for alignment: AI doesn't know your strategy, brand values, or technical constraints. Your curation ensures ideas align with organizational reality.

Reflection Questions

  1. Which category of ideas (MVP, radical, UX, business model) was most fruitful? Why?
  2. Did AI suggest anything you'd never considered? What made it novel?
  3. How will you balance "safe" MVP ideas with more experimental concepts?

Idea Refinement & Selection

From Idea Bank to prioritized concepts with rationale

Idea Refinement & Selection

Part 3 of 5

Goal

Combine AI-assisted scoring with human judgment to converge from 20-30 ideas down to two final concepts with clear rationale.

The Process

  1. AI Scoring: Rate each idea on feasibility and customer impact (1-5 scale)
  2. Visual Mapping: Plot ideas on a 2×2 matrix (Feasibility × Impact)
  3. Human Override: Debate, adjust scores, and record rationales
  4. Final Selection: Choose top two with documented reasoning

AI-Assisted Scoring

Scoring Prompt:
"Rate each of the following ideas on:
• Technical Feasibility (1=very hard, 5=easy)
• Customer Impact (1=low value, 5=transformative)

Provide a 1-2 sentence rationale for each score. Output as a table. Note any assumptions we should double-check."

Feasibility × Impact Matrix

High Impact, High Feasibility

Quick Wins: Pursue immediately

High Impact, Low Feasibility

Big Bets: Consider for long-term roadmap

Low Impact, High Feasibility

Fill-ins: Nice-to-haves if capacity exists

Low Impact, Low Feasibility

Deprioritize: Park for now

Activity: Score, Map, Select

⏱ 30 minutes total

AI Scoring + Matrix Mapping + Selection

Phase 1 (10 min): AI Scoring

  • Feed your Idea Bank to AI with the scoring prompt
  • Review AI's ratings and rationales
  • Challenge scores that seem off—ask AI to explain reasoning

Phase 2 (10 min): Matrix Mapping

  • Place each idea on the Feasibility × Impact grid in Mural
  • Use AI scores as starting point, adjust based on your context

Phase 3 (10 min): Selection & Rationale

  • Identify top 2 ideas (prioritize high-impact quadrants)
  • Document: Why these two? What trade-offs did you make?
  • Capture any ideas to revisit later (parking lot)

Key Takeaways: Refinement & Selection

Structured scoring reduces bias: Explicit criteria (feasibility, impact) prevent "loudest voice" or "personal favorite" from dominating decisions.

Visual mapping reveals trade-offs: The 2×2 matrix makes opportunity costs visible. Choosing one idea often means deferring another—document that decision.

Override with rationale, not hunches: When you disagree with AI scores, articulate why. "I have a gut feeling" is weak; "Our infrastructure doesn't support this" is actionable.

Selection is hypothesis, not certainty: You're choosing what to validate next, not predicting the future. Stay humble and plan to learn quickly.

Reflection Questions

  1. Where did AI scoring align with your intuition? Where did it diverge?
  2. What criteria beyond feasibility/impact mattered to your selection?
  3. How will you test whether your top two ideas truly have high impact?

Designing AI-Assisted Discovery Artifacts

From ideas to testable prototypes and research tools

AI-Assisted Discovery Artifacts

Part 4 of 5

Goal

Produce one low-fidelity discovery artifact (prototype sketch, interview guide, survey, or journey map) to validate your top idea.

The Process

  1. Choose artifact type aligned to your hypothesis
  2. Draft with AI to create scaffold quickly
  3. Refine for context: Adjust tone, scope, specificity
  4. Peer feedback via "glow & grow" review
  5. Reflect on process: How did AI help? Where did you add value?

Artifact Scaffolding Prompts

Interview Guide

"Draft a 6-question interview guide to validate [idea]. Questions should probe behaviors, constraints, and success signals."

Survey

"Produce a low-fi survey with 6-8 items (mix multiple choice and open-ended) to validate [hypothesis]."

Journey Map

"Sketch a journey map for [persona] attempting [goal] in NovaFit. Include stages, tasks, emotions, breakpoints."

Prototype Sketch

"Describe a low-fi prototype for [feature]. Include key screens, user flows, and interactions to test."

Refinement Checklist

  • ✓ Does it test your primary hypothesis?
  • ✓ Is the language appropriate for your target users?
  • ✓ Are questions/prompts open-ended enough to avoid bias?
  • ✓ Can you execute this in your timeframe and budget?

Activity: Design Your Artifact

⏱ 30 minutes total

Select → Draft → Refine → Review

Phase 1 (5 min): Artifact Selection

  • Pick one artifact type to validate your top idea
  • Write down the specific hypothesis you're testing

Phase 2 (15 min): AI Drafting + Refinement

  • Use the appropriate prompt to generate scaffold
  • Review AI output: adjust tone, add context-specific questions
  • Ensure artifact aligns with hypothesis and user needs

Phase 3 (5 min): Peer Feedback

  • Glow: What's working well?
  • Grow: What could be clearer or stronger?

Phase 4 (5 min): Process Reflection

  • How did AI accelerate your work? Where did you add critical value?

Key Takeaways: Discovery Artifacts

AI jump-starts, humans sharpen: AI gives you a draft in seconds. Your refinement—adjusting for tone, context, and hypothesis alignment—is where quality emerges.

Hypothesis clarity drives artifact quality: If you're fuzzy on what you're testing, even the best AI scaffold won't help. Crisp hypothesis = crisp artifact.

Peer review surfaces blind spots: You're close to your work; fresh eyes catch ambiguous questions, leading prompts, or missing elements.

Low-fi is a feature, not a bug: Polished artifacts invite feedback on aesthetics, not substance. Rough scaffolds focus conversation on the right things.

Reflection Questions

  1. What part of the artifact required the most human refinement? Why?
  2. How will you ensure your artifact doesn't bias responses toward a desired answer?
  3. When will you deploy this artifact, and with whom?

AI Reflection & Ethical Guardrails

Part 5 of 5

Goal

Map the benefits vs. risks of AI across discovery phases, and co-create 2-3 actionable "if-then" guardrails for responsible use.

The Process

  1. Map value: Where did AI add speed, creativity, or scale?
  2. Map risks: Bias, blind spots, over-reliance, hallucinations
  3. Note impacts: What happens if AI assumptions are wrong?
  4. Draft guardrails: Concrete "if-then" rules with examples from your work

Benefits vs. Risks Across Discovery

✅ Benefits

  • Speed: Hours → minutes for pattern mining and ideation
  • Scale: Process 100s of inputs without human bottleneck
  • Creativity: Surface ideas outside your usual frame
  • Consistency: Apply same criteria across all ideas
  • Documentation: Auto-generate rationales and records

⚠️ Risks

  • Bias amplification: AI reflects patterns in training data
  • Blind spots: Missing cultural context or emerging signals
  • Over-reliance: Accepting AI output without critical review
  • Hallucinations: Plausible-sounding but false information
  • Homogenization: Converging on "safe" ideas that AI favors

Real-World Impact Example

Scenario: AI analysis of NovaFit feedback emphasizes "busy professionals" pain points because that segment is over-represented in your data.

Consequence: You build features that exclude stay-at-home parents, students, or retirees—shrinking addressable market and missing diverse user needs.

Guardrail needed: Audit data sources for demographic balance before analysis.

Crafting "If-Then" Guardrails

Guardrails should be specific, actionable, and tied to concrete examples.

Example 1: Data Diversity

If qualitative data lacks representation from minority users or edge-case scenarios,

Then oversample interviews or surveys from those groups before running AI analysis.

Tie-in: NovaFit data skewed toward 30-40 age range; we'll add 10 interviews with 24-29 and 41-45 users.

Example 2: Human Override

If AI scoring conflicts with domain expertise or strategic priorities,

Then document the override rationale and review decision in next sprint retrospective.

Tie-in: AI rated "gamification badges" as low feasibility, but our eng team has a reusable system—we overrode to high feasibility.

Example 3: Validation Check

If AI generates a "fact" about user behavior or market data,

Then verify with primary sources (user interviews, analytics) before building on that assumption.

Tie-in: AI claimed "most users abandon fitness apps after 21 days"—we'll check our own retention curves before designing around this.

Example 4: Bias Audit

If a discovery artifact (interview guide, survey) was AI-drafted,

Then have someone from a different background review for leading language or cultural assumptions.

Tie-in: Our AI-generated interview guide used "gym" language; peer feedback noted not all cultures have gym-centric fitness norms.

Activity: Map & Mitigate

⏱ 25 minutes total

Benefits, Risks, Guardrails

Phase 1 (10 min): Map Benefits & Risks

  • In Mural "Ethics & Reflection" board, create two columns
  • List where AI added value in your work today (left column)
  • List potential risks or failure modes you noticed (right column)

Phase 2 (10 min): Draft Guardrails

  • Choose 2-3 risks that feel most relevant to your context
  • Write "if-then" guardrails using the template
  • Tie each to a concrete example from today's exercises

Phase 3 (5 min): Share & Refine

  • Share one guardrail with the group
  • Get feedback: Is it specific enough? Actionable?

Key Takeaways: Ethics & Guardrails

Balanced reflection, not fear: AI risks are real but manageable. The goal is informed use, not avoidance. Acknowledge both power and pitfalls.

Concrete > abstract: "Be careful with bias" is useless. "If data lacks X, then oversample Y" is actionable. Specificity drives compliance.

Guardrails evolve: Your first set won't be perfect. Revisit after each sprint or project. Learn from near-misses and update rules.

Ethics is a team sport: One person's guardrails aren't enough. Diverse perspectives catch blind spots. Make review collaborative and ongoing.

Reflection Questions

  1. Which risk feels most pressing in your organization? Why?
  2. How will you communicate these guardrails to stakeholders or team members?
  3. What process will you use to review and update guardrails over time?

Knowledge Check

Question 1: Ethical Guardrails

Which of the following is an effective "if-then" guardrail for AI-assisted discovery?

  • A) Always use AI for everything
  • B) Never trust AI outputs
  • C) If qualitative data lacks diversity, then oversample underrepresented groups before AI analysis
  • D) Only use AI for simple tasks

Correct Answer: C

Rationale: Effective guardrails are specific, actionable, and address concrete risks. Option C provides a clear trigger condition and mitigation action.

Question 2: Correlation to Action

NovaFit's AI analysis shows: "Users with sleep tracking enabled complete 30% more challenge workouts." What should you do FIRST?

  • A) Immediately build sleep tracking as the top priority feature
  • B) Validate the correlation with user interviews to understand causation
  • C) Ignore it—correlation doesn't matter
  • D) Remove sleep tracking from the app

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: Correlation ≠ causation. Validate with qualitative research before making product decisions. Perhaps motivated users enable all features, not that sleep tracking drives motivation.

Knowledge Check (Continued)

Question 3: Human Judgment + AI

During idea selection, AI rates a concept as "low feasibility" but your engineering team says it's easy given existing infrastructure. What should you do?

  • A) Trust AI—it has more data than your team
  • B) Override the AI score, document the rationale, and proceed with high feasibility
  • C) Discard the idea entirely
  • D) Ask AI again until it agrees with your team

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: Human expertise (especially context-specific like your tech stack) should override generic AI scoring. Document why for transparency and learning.

Question 4: Bias Spotting (Short Answer)

Name one potential bias that could emerge during AI-assisted problem discovery, and explain how you would catch it.

Sample Answer:

Bias: Selection bias—if your feedback data comes only from power users or vocal complainers, AI will surface problems specific to that group.

How to catch: Audit data sources for demographic and behavioral diversity. If a segment is missing, proactively gather input from that group before finalizing problem statements.
